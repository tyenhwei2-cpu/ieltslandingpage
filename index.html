How I Overcame My Accent Fear in IELTS Speaking

When I first started preparing for IELTS, I was terrified of my accent. I grew up in a small city in Guangdong, and my English teachers all had heavy Cantonese-influenced pronunciation. I thought my accent would cap me at Band 6.5 forever.

A solid grammar foundation gave me confidence to speak clearly.

I tried everything — YouTube pronunciation drills, shadowing native speakers, even recording myself and grimacing at the playback. But the real breakthrough came when I stopped trying to "erase" my accent and started focusing on clarity and rhythm.

I built a structured study plan using a mix of resources. Instead of obsessing over individual sounds, I practiced chunking words into natural thought groups. I also learned to use stress and intonation to signal meaning — which made me sound more fluent even when my vocabulary was simple.

Organised study materials — past papers, vocabulary books, and grammar guides.

One resource that really helped was English Grammar in Use — not for memorising rules, but for understanding how sentence structure affects clarity. When I stopped worrying about sounding "native" and started focusing on being understood, my speaking score jumped from 6.5 to 7.5 in three weeks.

A classic that taught me grammar is a tool, not a cage.
My biggest takeaway: The examiner doesn't want a BBC newsreader. They want someone who can communicate ideas clearly, confidently, and naturally. Your accent is part of your identity — embrace it, and focus on clarity.

If you're struggling with accent anxiety, my advice is simple: speak more, judge less. Record yourself, but listen for meaning, not perfection. And remember — every native speaker has an accent too.

How I Adapted My IELTS Speaking Strategy After the "Topic Pool" Was Scrapped

Frustration turned into determination — and a smarter strategy.

If you've ever found yourself drowning in a sea of IELTS speaking resources — countless past questions, topic lists, sample answers, and YouTube tip videos — you're not alone.

As an undergraduate student from a mainland China university applying for a Master program in Hong Kong, I needed a competitive overall band IELTS score. Speaking, however, remained my biggest unknown. I chose to self-study throughout as I don't have enough budget for expensive tutoring.

Like many other candidates, I started my preparation by studying the quarterly topic pools, especially for Part 2 narrative-style questions.

However, my journey took an unexpected turn just days before my test. The official IELTS announcement to discontinue the fixed quarterly topic pool rendered weeks of targeted preparation essentially useless. This transformed my approach to IELTS speaking. Here is what I learned, and how I adapted — not by memorizing more, but by preparing smarter.

To tackle the new situation, I combined two distinct but complementary modes to build both fluency and confidence.

  1. AI as a Thinking Partner — Whenever I encountered a new topic and felt stuck, I used AI to help map out logical structures (e.g., deciding which angle to take, how to pace my story). When I lacked a relevant personal experience, I used AI to generate natural, everyday scenarios that felt genuine and conversational.
  2. Human Simulation for Real-Time Agility — I scheduled regular practice sessions with a friend who would act as the examiner. This helped me: get used to thinking on my feet, manage the anxiety of being watched and listened to in real time, and improve my ability to maintain eye contact, use natural pauses, and adjust my tone — all the non-verbal cues that matter on test day.

Walking out of the test room, my biggest takeaway was this: over-preparation in the form of complex vocabulary and elaborate sentence structures can do more harm than good.

Many candidates invest excessive energy in memorizing high-level words or crafting "impressive" grammatical constructions. Yet, the core assessment criteria — Fluency and Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range, and Pronunciation — don't reward difficulty for its own sake. They reward clarity, relevance, and ease of delivery.

A Band 7+ response is not one that sounds like a written essay. It is one that sounds like a thoughtful, organized, and confident person speaking naturally.

Based on my experience, here are four practical takeaways that I believe any self-study candidate can adopt:

  1. Ditch the Full Scripts; Master the Outline — Instead of memorizing paragraphs, practice creating a simple mental outline for any topic — your opening statement, 2–3 key points or chronological events, and a closing reflection. This structure keeps you anchored without boxing you in.
  2. Use AI as a Support Tool — Leverage generative AI to brainstorm angles, refine awkward phrasing, or tailor examples to your real-life context. Think of it as a collaborative editor, not an answer generator.
  3. Speak Out Loud — Every Day — Writing answers is not the same as speaking them. Practice responding to random questions (from any source, not just recent pools) under timed conditions. Record yourself and listen back to identify patterns — repetition, filler words, or unclear pronunciation.
  4. Prioritize Fluency Over Fancy Vocabulary — Focus on being understood and maintaining a steady, natural rhythm. Use vocabulary you are fully comfortable with. When you hesitate, it breaks the flow; when you speak fluidly, even with simpler words, the examiner can follow your argument effortlessly.

The era of relying on a predictable question bank is behind us. That is not a disadvantage — it's an invitation to build real speaking competence that goes beyond a single exam. Flexible, principle-based preparation is not just better for your test score; it's better for your confidence and your communication skills in the long run.

If you are currently preparing, I hope this post helps you avoid the panic of sudden changes and instead focus on what truly matters: thinking clearly, speaking naturally, and trusting your ability to communicate.
